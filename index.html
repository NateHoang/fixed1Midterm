Lecture 1: Posterior Crowns
Aspect Details
Definition of Treatment Planning A logical sequence of treatment designed to restore the patient's dentition to good health, optimal function, and appearance. Must account for patient preferences and needs.
Conditions for Crown Treatment

A crown must achieve one or more of:

  1. Correct Existing Disease
  2. Prevent Future Disease
  3. Restore Function
  4. Improve Appearance
Partial vs. Full Coverage Partial coverage (onlay) relies on residual tooth strength. Full coverage reinforces weakened teeth and re-establishes ideal occlusion. Traditional rule: if filling extends > 1/3 of occlusal table, crown recommended (modern porcelain may alter this).
Clinical Examples
  • Large DOL amalgam with distobuccal cusp broken off → minimal residual structure → build-up + full coverage crown.
  • Amalgam occupying most of occlusal table → all cusps need coverage (onlay, crownlay, or full coverage). Insurance often covers full coverage.
Wedging Effect In severely weakened teeth, intracoronal restorations generate greater wedging forces → tooth fracture. Full coverage avoids this.
Aspect Details
Periodontal Foundation The periodontium is the foundation; if compromised, the crown will fail regardless of quality. Key factors: attachment loss and furcation exposure.
Prognosis Tooth with 50% attachment loss and Class 3 furcation → poor overall prognosis. Crown placement questionable.
Crown-to-Root Ratio Physical relationship between portion of tooth within alveolar bone vs. not within bone. Determined via radiograph. Desired ratio < 1 (more tooth in bone). Ideal ratio 2:3 (Tooth A). Ratio > 1 (Tooth B) → poor long-term prognosis.
Aspect Details
Crown Margin The outer edge of the crown. Interacts with the finish line (boundary surface of tooth preparation), also called finish curve.
Margin Placement Options
  1. Supra-gingival – above gum line.
  2. Crestal – at gum crest.
  3. Sub-gingival – below gum line.
Factors Influencing Placement
  • Decay location
  • Margin of old restoration/crown
  • Material used
  • Emergence profile
  • Patient esthetics
Margin Shape Should follow the shape of the CEJ – higher interproximally than on buccal/lingual. Must be smooth (minimal irregularities) for crown adaptation, impression, lab steps, scanning, milling.
“J” Shaped Margin (Ski Jump) Caused by sinking a rounded diamond beyond the width of the cutting tip → creates unsupported enamel. Remove with end cutting diamond. Prevention: use multi-radius diamond (our main bur in bur block).
Final Margin Shape Butt joint with 90° exit angle. Lingual margin = flat butt joint → reduces stress. Buccal margin sloped → stress concentration → fracture/failure (acceptable for PFM with metal support).
Step Description
1. Buccal & Lingual Axial Reduction Reduce vertical walls on buccal and lingual surfaces.
2. Interproximal Reduction Connect buccal and lingual reductions mesially and distally.
3. Occlusal Reduction Reduce occlusal surface following tooth planes.
4. Functional Cusp Reduction/Bevel Create a third plane on the functional cusp for clearance.
5. Smoothing & Refining Smooth all surfaces and margins.
6. Final Evaluation/Assessment Check for undercuts, margin quality, axial depth, occlusal clearance.
Aspect Details
Taper Convergence of two opposing external walls (e.g., M-D or B-L) as viewed in a given plane. Ideal taper = 6° (acceptable 10°–12°). Achieved by holding diamond bur in constant angle.
Line of Draw (Path of Insertion) The path a crown follows to seat onto the tooth. Should be consistent and generally aligned with the long axis of the tooth.
Aspect Details
Definition Reduction of vertical walls (buccal, lingual, mesial, distal).
Material-Specific Depth at Margin
  • Porcelain: 1.0–1.5 mm
  • PFM: 1.0–1.25 mm
  • Gold: 0.5 mm
Technique
  1. Make depth cuts/grooves on B and L surfaces using chamfer diamond, round bur, or carbide. Apical portion slightly above final margin location.
  2. Hold bur in long axis of tooth to generate taper.
  3. Connect depth grooves for continuous reduction.
Appearance Initially rough and jagged; smoothed later.
Aspect Details
Purpose Connect buccal and lingual axial reductions.
Two Techniques
  1. Brushing motion from buccal to lingual.
  2. Prepare down from occlusal like an interproximal filling.
Important Anatomy Tooth is 1.5–2.0 mm wider at the contact point than at the CEJ. This prevents damaging the adjacent tooth during preparation.
Aspect Details
Principle Follow the planes of the tooth (morphological reduction) to preserve tooth structure and provide proper clearance.
Occlusal Clearance Amount of reduction needed for adequate thickness of restorative material. Requires functional cusp bevel for proper clearance.
Morphological Reduction Reduce occlusion while following differing planes of occlusal table (triangular ridges → grooves → fossa).
Technique
  1. Make depth grooves in occlusal table using chamfer diamond or depth cutting bur.
  2. Connect grooves with chamfer diamond following tooth planes.
High/Low Points Triangular ridges and oblique ridge (maxillary molar) = high points; grooves = low points. Depth grooves placed at same plane angle as buccal cusp.
Final Appearance Should mimic the anatomy of the occlusal table; tooth type should be identifiable.
Aspect Details
Definition A third plane of occlusal reduction on the functional cusp. NOT a composite bevel (0.5–1.0 mm). It is large and follows the angulation of the tooth.
Which Cusp?
  • Maxilla: Lingual cusp (lingual-occlusal surface).
  • Mandible: Buccal cusp (buccal-occlusal surface).
Purpose Provides adequate clearance for restorative material on the functional cusp that contacts the opposing tooth's marginal ridge or central fossa.
Visualization From interproximal view: axial wall (blue) + occlusal reduction (green) + functional cusp bevel (red) = three distinct planes. The bevel connects axial wall to occlusal table.
Size Only slightly smaller than the other two occlusal planes.
Technique Angulate bur to follow the tooth's surface angulation. On mandibular teeth: buccal surface of buccal cusp. On maxillary: lingual surface of lingual cusps.
Aspect Details
Smoothing & Refining Smooth all prepared surfaces and margins.
Evaluation for Undercuts View from occlusal; look for irregularities that prevent seating/removal. Smooth with chamfer diamond.
Margin Evaluation Check smoothness visually or with explorer. If rough, smooth with end cutting diamond or chamfer diamond without water with gentle touch.
Axial Depth Check Ensure correct depth circumferentially.
Finish Line Contour Follow gingival contours – interproximal margin should be higher occlusally than buccal/lingual.
Occlusal Clearance Evaluation
  1. Visual (least accurate).
  2. Prep check devices (instruments or bite tabs) – tip should move freely, tabs slide out in MIP.
  3. Rigid bite registration material – patient bites in MIP, set, measure thickness with calipers.
Aspect Details
Common Sites of Damage
  • Adjacent tooth
  • Soft tissue (gums, tongue, cheek)
  • Pulp
Soft Tissue Protection Rubber dam usually not used; dentist and assistant must monitor patient's tongue and cheeks.
Adjacent Tooth Damage Most common during interproximal axial reduction. Remember: teeth are 1.5–2.0 mm wider at contact than at CEJ. Consider this when starting axial reduction.
Material Advantages Disadvantages Reduction Requirements
All-Ceramic (Emax, Zirconia)
  • Higher esthetics
  • Natural appearance
  • Emax and zirconia less brittle
  • No metal substructure
  • More brittle (improved with modern materials)
  • Greater occlusal and axial reduction required
  • Porcelain occlusal more abrasive to opposing teeth
  • Occlusal: 2 mm (Emax); all zirconia can be 1 mm (prone to fracture).
  • Margin depth: at least 1 mm → axial reduction ≥ 1.5 mm mid-wall.
  • All areas rounded.
  • Same for PFZ (e.g., LAVA).
PFM (Porcelain Fused to Metal)
  • Greater support from metal substructure
  • Metal occlusal less abrasive
  • Less esthetic (graying effect)
  • Porcelain-metal interface can fail
  • Porcelain needs metal support
  • With porcelain occlusion: proper B/L reduction for thickness without overcontour.
  • With metal occlusion & lingual margin: occlusal 1.5 mm; buccal 1–1.5 mm; lingual 0.5–0.6 mm.
Gold
  • Minimal reduction required
  • Durable
 Not esthetic. Axial reduction: 0.5 mm at margin.
Parameter Value
Ideal taper 6° (acceptable 10°–12°)
Crown-to-root ratio ideal 2:3 (ratio < 1)
Tooth width difference (contact vs. CEJ) 1.5–2.0 mm
Axial reduction at margin – Porcelain 1.0–1.5 mm
Axial reduction at margin – PFM 1.0–1.25 mm
Axial reduction at margin – Gold 0.5 mm
Occlusal reduction – All-ceramic 2.0 mm (Emax); 1.0 mm (zirconia)
Occlusal reduction – PFM metal occlusion 1.5 mm
Buccal reduction – PFM metal occlusion 1.0–1.5 mm
Lingual reduction – PFM metal occlusion 0.5–0.6 mm
Margin exit angle 90° (butt joint)
